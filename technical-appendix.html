Quick start for non-technical users

  1. Go to Settings and confirm the planning horizon, discount rate, completion rate, and outbreak values.
  2. Go to Configuration and choose programme tier, mentorship intensity, delivery mode, number of cohorts, trainees per cohort, and cost per trainee per month.
  3. Click Apply configuration to update Results, Costing and National results.
  4. Click Save scenario to store it, then build 2–4 alternative scenarios (for example, low vs high mentorship, different cohorts, blended vs online).
  5. Go to Scenarios to compare saved scenarios, shortlist your preferred options and export Excel/PDF (Standard or Brief 2 pages).
  6. Use Copilot / ChatGPT briefing to produce a policy-ready summary for meetings.
Best practice for meetings: shortlist 3 scenarios: one “baseline”, one “ambitious scale”, and one “high-quality mentorship” scenario. Export the Brief PDF and use the assumptions box as a slide backup.

What STEPS is (and what it is not)

STEPS is a decision aid

STEPS helps you compare alternative FETP scale-up configurations. It combines: (i) a preference model that predicts endorsement and perceived programme value; (ii) transparent costing (direct vs economic costs) including mentor support; and (iii) an indicative epidemiological benefits module.

  • It is designed for transparency: assumptions are visible and exports can stand alone.
  • It supports scenario planning: you can save options and compare them side by side.
  • It supports feasibility: it estimates mentor needs and flags capacity gaps.

STEPS is a decision-support and scenario-analysis tool

The tool does not claim to predict real-world outbreaks or budgets with certainty. It provides structured comparisons under stated assumptions, so decision-makers can discuss trade-offs clearly.

Important: results are only as credible as the assumptions (costs, completion rates, outbreaks per graduate, value per outbreak) you input. Always treat outputs as scenario-based estimates, not guarantees.

STEPS outputs are best used alongside operational planning (NCDC staffing plans, site readiness assessments, faculty/mentor rosters, and budget constraints).

What each tab means

Overview

A plain-language introduction to the purpose of the tool and what each module does. Use this tab to orient new meeting participants.

Framework

Explains the policy levers and programme attributes represented in STEPS (tier, incentives, mentorship intensity, delivery mode, response time, cost). These match the attributes used in the preference study that underpins endorsement and perceived programme value.

Settings

Global assumptions used for all scenarios. These include epidemiological values (outbreak responses per graduate, value per outbreak), planning horizon, discount rate, completion rate, opportunity cost settings, mentor cost base, capacity inputs and the One Health / cross-sector multiplier.

Settings are intentionally visible because exported results should be interpretable without opening the tool.

Configuration

Where you build one scenario at a time. Choose programme tier and design features, specify scale (cohorts and trainees), and specify the direct cost per trainee per month (INR). The opportunity cost switch determines whether economic costs include trainee time cost.

Click Apply configuration to compute results. Click Save scenario to store the scenario for comparison and export.

Results

The main interpretation panel. It reports endorsement, opt-out share, perceived programme value outputs, cost totals, epidemiological benefits and feasibility checks.

Use this tab for discussion: “What do stakeholders value?”, “What is the scale implication?”, “Do we have enough mentors?”, and “Do benefits exceed costs under these assumptions?”

Costing

Shows direct cost, economic cost and opportunity cost breakdowns per cohort and nationally (all cohorts). Direct costs exclude opportunity cost; economic costs include it when the toggle is ON.

National results

Aggregates across all cohorts. Useful for national planning discussions: total costs and total epidemiological benefits over the planning horizon, plus net benefit and BCR at national scale.

Perceived programme value / Sensitivity

Allows you to explore how results change if epidemiological assumptions change (for example, outbreak value or outbreak responses per graduate). It can also show different “benefit definitions” (for example, outbreak benefits only, or perceived value plus outbreak benefits) to support deliberation.

Copilot / ChatGPT briefing

Generates a pre-filled prompt that includes your selected scenarios, assumptions and feasibility outputs. The goal is to produce a policy-ready brief (or slide-ready summary) with a compact table and plain-language narrative.

Scenarios

A comparison table for saved scenarios. You can shortlist scenarios for export, view snapshots, and export Excel/PDF. The “Brief (2 pages)” PDF is designed specifically for meetings.

Key concepts explained

Endorsement and opt-out

Endorsement is the predicted share of stakeholders who would choose a configured FETP option rather than an opt-out alternative, based on the preference study. Opt-out share is 100% minus endorsement.

How to interpret: If endorsement is high, the option aligns well with stakeholder preferences (for example, higher mentorship intensity may increase endorsement even if costs rise).

Technical note: endorsement is computed from the preference model (mixed logit or MNL) using the chosen attributes and cost.

Perceived programme value

STEPS reports Perceived programme value per trainee per month. This is an implicit valuation derived from stated preferences in the discrete choice experiment. It is not a fee, and no one is expected to pay it. It is a way to summarise how much stakeholders value the package of programme features, on a rupee scale.

Interpretation rule: use perceived programme value to compare options (relative differences), not as a procurement price or a budget request.

STEPS also reports total perceived programme value per cohort (and total across all cohorts), which is computed by scaling the per-trainee-per-month value by cohort size and programme duration.

Direct costs vs economic costs (including mentor cost)

STEPS separates costs into:

  • Direct cost: financial cost of delivery (plus mentor support cost), excluding opportunity cost.
  • Economic cost: direct cost plus opportunity cost of trainee time when the opportunity cost switch is ON.

A new explicit component is Mentor support cost. In Settings you input a base cost per cohort, and STEPS applies a multiplier by mentorship intensity: Low = 1.0, Medium = 1.3, High = 1.7.

Epidemiological benefits

Epidemiological benefits represent the value of improved outbreak responses enabled by FETP graduates over the planning horizon. The structure is intentionally simple and transparent:

  • Compute expected graduates = cohorts × trainees per cohort × completion rate.
  • Compute annual outbreak responses supported = graduates × outbreaks per graduate per year.
  • Compute outbreak value = annual outbreaks supported × value per outbreak, projected across the planning horizon, discounted by the discount rate.

If you use the Cross-sector benefit multiplier (One Health), STEPS multiplies epidemiological benefits by that factor (for example, 1.2 means “20% higher benefits to reflect cross-sector spillovers”). This multiplier does not change endorsement or perceived programme value.

Capacity and feasibility checks

STEPS estimates mentor needs based on cohort size and mentorship intensity. Mentor capacity assumptions:

  • Low mentorship: 5 fellows per mentor
  • Medium mentorship: 3.5 fellows per mentor
  • High mentorship: 2 fellows per mentor

It then computes required mentors per cohort and nationally:

  • Required mentors per cohort = ceil(trainees per cohort ÷ mentor capacity)
  • Required total mentors nationally = required mentors per cohort × number of cohorts

If required mentors exceed Available mentors nationally (Settings), STEPS flags “Requires capacity expansion” and shows the shortfall. Optional training sites inputs can flag whether cohort counts exceed site throughput.

How to use this: feasibility flags are not “stop signs”; they are prompts for planning (mentor recruitment, hub expansion, phased rollout).

BCR and net benefit

STEPS computes:

  • Net benefit = total epidemiological benefits − total economic costs
  • Benefit-cost ratio (BCR) = total epidemiological benefits ÷ total economic costs

Interpretation:

  • If net benefit is positive, benefits exceed costs under the chosen assumptions.
  • If BCR > 1, benefits exceed costs in ratio terms.
  • Use sensitivity analysis to see how robust these outputs are to uncertain assumptions.

Assumptions and recommended ranges guidance

STEPS is built to make assumptions explicit. Below are practical suggestions for setting and interpreting each key parameter. The “right” value depends on programme design, context and the decision question.

Assumption What it means How it affects results Practical guidance
Planning horizon (years) How many years you project benefits and costs. Longer horizons increase total benefits (and can increase total costs if you interpret costs annually). Use 5–10 years for typical planning; use 10+ for longer health security investment cases.
Discount rate (%) Reduces the present value of future costs and benefits. Higher discount rates reduce long-horizon benefits more strongly. Use the rate aligned with government guidance. Test 3% and 5% in sensitivity.
Completion rate (%) Share of trainees completing and becoming graduates. Higher completion → more graduates → higher benefits. Use observed completion rates from prior cohorts, by tier if available.
Outbreak responses per graduate per year How many major outbreak responses one graduate supports annually. Directly scales benefits. Use conservative values unless evidence supports higher. Test a low and high scenario.
Value per outbreak (INR) Monetary value of improved response to a large outbreak. Directly scales benefits; biggest driver of net benefit. Use literature or country estimates when available; otherwise include a range in sensitivity (for example, ₹10m–₹50m).
Non-outbreak value per graduate (optional) Annual value from surveillance and routine contributions. Adds benefits even outside outbreaks. Only use if you have defensible evidence; otherwise keep at 0 and discuss qualitatively.
Opportunity cost per trainee per month Value of trainee time (what they could do otherwise). Increases economic cost when toggle is ON. Use payroll-based estimates or conservative time-value proxies; toggle OFF if presenting purely budget costs.
Mentor support cost per cohort (base) Explicit cost of mentorship support per cohort (supervision time, travel, honoraria, coordination). Raises direct and economic costs; scaled by mentorship intensity multiplier. Set based on actual mentorship programme budgets. If uncertain, test ±25% and compare feasibility.
Cross-sector benefit multiplier Applies only to epidemiological benefits to reflect One Health/cross-sector spillovers. Scales benefits, not preference outputs. Use 1.0 by default; only increase with a clear rationale and document it in notes.
Available mentors nationally Current mentor pool capacity. Determines feasibility status and shortfall. Use roster-based counts of active mentors. Update yearly.
Decision tip: if a scenario has excellent endorsement and benefits but fails feasibility checks, treat it as a “target state” and design a phased plan (for example, medium mentorship first, expand mentor pool, then move to high mentorship).

Step 1: Programme delivery cost per cohort

Delivery cost per cohort = cost per trainee per month × trainees per cohort × duration in months. If duration is 12 months: 

Delivery cost per cohort = 60,000 × 35 × 12 = 25,200,000 INR

Step 2: Mentor cost per cohort

Mentor cost per cohort = base mentor cost × mentorship multiplier. 

Mentor cost per cohort = 500,000 × 1.3 = 650,000 INR

Step 3: Direct cost per cohort

Direct cost per cohort = delivery cost + mentor cost
= 25,200,000 + 650,000
= 25,850,000 INR

Step 4: Economic cost per cohort

If opportunity cost toggle is ON, add trainee time value (opportunity cost per trainee per month × trainees × duration). Suppose opportunity cost per trainee per month = 25,000 INR: 

Opportunity cost per cohort = 25,000 × 35 × 12 = 10,500,000 INR
Economic cost per cohort = direct cost + opportunity cost
= 25,850,000 + 10,500,000
= 36,350,000 INR

Step 5: Graduates over the horizon

Total trainees = 10 × 35 = 350
Expected graduates = 350 × 0.85 = 297.5 ≈ 298 graduates

Step 6: Outbreak responses supported per year

Annual outbreaks supported = graduates × outbreaks per graduate
= 298 × 0.8 ≈ 238.4 outbreaks per year

Step 7: Outbreak benefits (discounted)

In a simple approximation without discounting: 

Annual outbreak value ≈ 238.4 × 25,000,000 ≈ 5,960,000,000 INR per year

STEPS discounts future benefits over the planning horizon using the discount rate. The discounted total will be lower than “annual × years”. Discounting matters most when horizons are long.

Step 8: Net benefit and BCR

National economic cost = economic cost per cohort × cohorts: 

National economic cost = 36,350,000 × 10 = 363,500,000 INR

Net benefit and BCR: 

Net benefit = total discounted benefits − total economic cost
BCR = total discounted benefits ÷ total economic cost
How to use the worked example: the goal is to understand the structure. In the tool, exports list all assumptions and computed values so you can replicate the logic transparently.

Technical details (for analysts)

1) Preference model outputs: endorsement and perceived programme value

STEPS uses a preference model (Mixed Logit baseline, with an optional MNL). The inputs are attribute levels selected in the Configuration tab plus the cost level.

  • Endorsement is computed from the model’s choice probabilities for the configured alternative relative to opt-out.
  • Perceived programme value per trainee per month is computed by translating attribute utility into a rupee metric using the cost coefficient (i.e., a “willingness-to-pay style” conversion). In UI and exports we label this explicitly as perceived value derived from stated preferences.
Analyst caution: do not treat perceived programme value as a market price or a budget. It is a welfare/valuation signal from stated preference data.

2) Cost functions

Delivery cost per cohort is computed from:

delivery_cost_per_cohort = cost_per_trainee_per_month × trainees_per_cohort × duration_months(tier)

Mentor cost per cohort is computed from:

mentor_multiplier = {low:1.0, medium:1.3, high:1.7}[mentorship]
mentor_cost_per_cohort = base_mentor_cost_per_cohort × mentor_multiplier

Direct cost per cohort:

direct_cost_per_cohort = delivery_cost_per_cohort + mentor_cost_per_cohort

Opportunity cost per cohort (applied only if toggle ON):

opp_cost_per_cohort = opp_cost_per_trainee_per_month × trainees_per_cohort × duration_months(tier)

Economic cost per cohort:

economic_cost_per_cohort = direct_cost_per_cohort + (opp_toggle ? opp_cost_per_cohort : 0)

3) Epidemiological module and discounting

Conceptually: 

graduates = cohorts × trainees_per_cohort × completion_rate
annual_outbreaks_supported = graduates × outbreaks_per_graduate_per_year
annual_benefit = annual_outbreaks_supported × value_per_outbreak
total_discounted_benefit = Σ_{t=1..H} (annual_benefit / (1 + r)^t)

If non-outbreak value is used, it is included as an additional annual stream per graduate (or graduate-years) depending on the tool’s internal implementation.

Cross-sector multiplier is applied to epidemiological benefits only: 

epi_benefit_adjusted = epi_benefit × cross_sector_multiplier

4) Capacity check

mentor_capacity = {low:5, medium:3.5, high:2}[mentorship]
mentors_per_cohort = ceil(trainees_per_cohort / mentor_capacity)
total_mentors_required = mentors_per_cohort × cohorts
shortfall = max(0, total_mentors_required − available_mentors)

5) Feasibility status logic

A simple flag: 

if shortfall == 0:
  status = "Within current capacity"
else:
  status = "Requires capacity expansion"

Optional site capacity: 

if sites > 0 and max_cohorts_per_site > 0:
  site_capacity = sites × max_cohorts_per_site
  site_gap = max(0, cohorts − site_capacity)

6) National aggregation

national_cost = economic_cost_per_cohort × cohorts
national_benefit = total_discounted_benefit (already based on total graduates)
national_net_benefit = national_benefit − national_cost
national_bcr = national_benefit / national_cost

7) Sensitivity analysis

Sensitivity recomputes benefits and net benefits under alternative assumptions while holding preference model outputs constant. Benefit definitions exist to support decision discussions, not to assert one “correct” welfare metric.

FAQ and interpretation tips

Why can endorsement be high while net benefit is low?

Endorsement reflects stakeholder preferences (including quality features), while net benefit depends mainly on cost and epidemiological value assumptions. A high-quality, high-mentorship scenario may be preferred but expensive; if outbreak values are set conservatively, net benefits can appear low.

How should we choose between direct cost and economic cost?

Use direct cost when planning budgets and financial feasibility. Use economic cost when presenting a broader societal perspective that includes trainee time. STEPS always reports both; exports show whether opportunity cost is included.

What does “Perceived programme value” mean in meetings?

It means “how much stakeholders value the configuration”, expressed in rupee terms via the preference model. Use it as a communication device for preference strength; do not treat it as a procurement or payroll number.

What if the capacity check says ‘Requires capacity expansion’?

That is a planning prompt: consider (i) phased rollout; (ii) mentor recruitment/training; (iii) hub expansion; or (iv) selecting a mentorship intensity that is feasible today. Save alternative scenarios to compare trade-offs.

How should we use the cross-sector multiplier?

Leave it at 1.0 unless you have a defensible reason to incorporate cross-sector spillovers (e.g., animal health, environmental surveillance). If you raise it above 1.0, explain the rationale in the export notes and sensitivity tab.

Good practice for meetings and decision-making

  • Always export with assumptions visible. Use the Brief PDF for meetings; keep Excel for analysts.
  • Use scenarios to structure trade-offs. At minimum compare: baseline, scale expansion, and high-mentorship quality scenario.
  • Interpret the preference model as “support”, not “truth”. Endorsement and perceived programme value represent preferences under the study design.
  • Test robustness. Use sensitivity to check how results change with different outbreak values and completion assumptions.
  • Use feasibility flags to design implementation plans. Shortfalls are actionable: mentor pipeline, hub planning, staged cohorts.
Meeting template suggestion: Start with the Brief PDF. Discuss (1) endorsement, (2) costs and feasibility, (3) benefits and net benefit under baseline assumptions, then (4) sensitivity ranges.
